Host Ryan VanGrack joined Parallel on a timely discussion about the potential ban of prediction markets. Paul Barron, a Coinbase lawyer, shed light on critical and timely issues affecting the industry.

According to Paul, the conversation started with a video featuring Matt Hogan, where Paul asked him about being exposed to pre-stocks, and Matt mentioned a 5% exposure as a recommended diversification. Paul then discussed how prediction markets, such as calcium and polymarket, are changing the game. Polymarket is leading the way in crypto and sports, but calcium is still popular. Paul thinks polymarket will explode again, particularly during the midterms, and notes that Polymarket partnered with the NHL, offering no-fee, no-house, and no-limit trades. Paul also mentioned that Polymarket is paying users some money on their money, while calcium is not.

Paul said that if you look at the valuations on pre-stocks, you can see polymarket and calcium competing. Polymarket is a great example because it's pushing the DJNs (derivatives market participants) to Polymarket, making a better case for their competitor. Paul also highlighted the importance of fees in prediction markets, which is something he thinks will be huge going forward. He believes that prediction markets will become huge, but notes that there's a debate about whether they should be regulated by the states or the CFTC.

Paul explained that the CFTC has authority to regulate prediction markets under the CFTC's Exchange Act. He believes that the CFTC should regulate these markets because they involve derivatives and event contracts, which require national regulation. However, Paul also thinks that there's a need for regulation, citing the issue of Kalshi's contract and the controversy surrounding it. According to Paul, there's a process for reconciling contracts, and Kalshi has committed to reimbursing customers.

Ryan VanGrack pointed out that Chris Christie, a prominent figure, is speaking out against prediction markets, saying they're not the house. Paul argues that Christie frames it wrong, suggesting that the real question is whether prediction markets should be regulated by the state or the federal government. Paul explains that prediction markets are different from sports books, and he highlights four key differences: price discovery, the incentive structure, the type of contracts used, and the ability to enter and exit positions before settlement.

According to Ryan, Paul makes a strong argument that prediction markets should be regulated by the CFTC. Ryan agrees that these markets have more experience and precedent in the securities space, but notes that similar concepts should apply to insider trading and market manipulation rules. Ryan points out that there's not enough case law to deal with insider trading, and the regulatory framework is still developing.

Paul discussed the legal differences between social utility and entertainment consumption, noting that the question is not about social utility, but whether there are potential economic, financial, or commercial consequences. Paul believes that prediction markets have significant potential financial consequences and should be treated as such. He also talked about the oil market and Palantir stock, noting that these markets are not suggesting that they should be shut down.

Paul concluded that the key to addressing the issue is to separate fact from fiction, and he encourages listeners to use platforms like his to do so. He believes that at the end of the day, facts in the law will prevail.
